Why don't american speed limit signs have a unit?

November 27, 2025 • Reading time: 6 min

Road signs play a crucial role in maintaining safety, guiding drivers, and reducing accidents on the road. Whether you're preparing for a driving test, designing manuals, or creating traffic-related visuals, understanding the different types of road signs is essential. This guide explains the major categories with examples and use cases.

Why Don’t American Speed Limit Signs Show a Unit?

If you’ve ever driven in the United States, you might have noticed something unusual about speed limit signs :they simply say a number, like “55” or “70”, without mentioning miles per hour (mph). For many visitors, this can be confusing. So why do American signs skip the unit?

1. Historical Standardization

The U.S. has been using the imperial system for measurements since its early days. When speed limits were first introduced, it was assumed that everyone understood that the numbers were in miles per hour. There was no need to write “mph” because it was considered common knowledge.

2. Simplicity and Visibility

Road signs need to be read quickly by drivers. Adding extra text like “mph” could make the sign cluttered and harder to read at high speeds. By keeping the sign simple, drivers can understand it instantly and react safely.

3. Legal Clarity

In U.S. traffic law, all speed limits are defined in miles per hour. This means the number alone is sufficient for enforcement and legal purposes. There’s no ambiguity — the number always refers to mph unless stated otherwise.

4. Contrast With Other Countries

Many countries that use the metric system, such as Canada or European nations, display km/h on speed limit signs to prevent confusion. Since the U.S. does not use kilometers per hour widely, the unit is simply omitted.

5. International Drivers Are Expected to Learn Local Rules

While foreign drivers may wonder about the missing unit, international driving guidelines expect drivers to know local conventions. Signage in the U.S. assumes familiarity with the imperial system.

Conclusion

American speed limit signs are simple, clear, and rooted in history, law, and practicality. By omitting the unit, signs remain easy to read at a glance, while drivers in the U.S. inherently understand that the numbers represent miles per hour.

So next time you see a “65” on a U.S. highway sign, remember — it’s all about clarity and efficiency, not laziness.
